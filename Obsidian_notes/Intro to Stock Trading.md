**Definition: P/E'S:** It stands for **"Price to earning ratio"**. It is an indicator of how good a company is doing and it helps to select good companies to invest. Companies in *troubles* has a P/E lower than 10, companies that are *growing* and *doing good* have P/E of 25 or higher.
***How to compute it?*** Let's say that a company has ***E***arnings ***P***er ***S***hare (***EPS***) of $6.50 over the last 12 months and the stock trades for $100. 
$$
P/E = \frac{Stock \;Price}{Earnings \;Per \; Share} = \frac{P}{EPS}
$$
so for this case it will be 100/6.5=15, which is a good value for a company.
*According to the previous equation* for a company to have a good P/E it should have a good stock price and a lower earning per share.

* By using this metric an investor can see the value of a stock in terms of how much the market is willing to pay for each dollar or earning 
* ***Why is this important*** 
Let's consider the cases in which P/E can be high:
1. That EPS is low and P is high. In this case a company doesn't have high earnings per share but people will pay a lot for shares. This doesn't happen in real life.
2. In fact, a good company is expected to have a high EPS, so in order to have a high P/E it means that the stock price has to be higher than the EPS.  

**Definition: Earnings Per Share (EPS):** It is calculated a company's profit divided by the outstanding shares of its common stock. ***The higher a company's EPS, the more profitable it is considered to be***. It indicates how much money a company makes for each of its stock and is a widely used metric for estimating corporate value.
* A higher EPS indicates that investors will pay more for a company's shares if they think the company has higher profits relatives to its share price
* The way to calculate it is dividing the net income (profits or earnings) by the amount of available shares

### Long and short

Normally when you buy stocks you want the price go up from the price you bought. But when you **short a stock** we need it to *go down from the price* that we sold it. 
**How does short work?** you sell stocks that are not yours but you borrow them from a firm with an agreement to pay for them at market price at a later date with the hope that the price of the stock will go down at a later time.
==in my own words== Let's say I borrow stocks on Monday from a certain firm and I agree with them to pay for those stocks at market price on Friday. So I check during the week and I decide to sell those stock on Thursday for $100, then when Friday comes the market price for those stocks was $90, which gave me $10 profit.

**Longing stocks** The act of buying shares of a stock with the hopes that the price will increase and you will be able to eventually sell them for more than you paid for them.

**Definition** ***IPO*** Initial Public Offering. It happens when a formerly private company decides to take on outside investors.
*To take into account* when buying IPO's there is the risk that people from inside the company are trying to cash out their money and maybe the company is not doing so great. So just consider how the company operates and get well informed about it.

2. *From a trader's perspective* An IPO with a small float has the potential to go up or down a lot, which makes it a great trading vehicle. Try to look for no hype companies and the ones no sot well known but with a good projection.

## A Day Trading Strategy That Actually Works

It is about any strategy that *involves buying and selling a stock on the same day*. A day trader will never hold a position overnight. 
This strategy takes advantage of a ***basic fact of market structure*** ==It takes time for big players to enter and exit their positions== When millions of shared from a stock are being hold it will take even week to leave them (exit its position) , depending on how liquid the stock is. The good news is that small traders can take advantage of this. Once we see their footprints in a stock's price behavior, we can step in front of them and quickly execute our small order. Our order won't pull the stock in any direction. 

By jumping in front of a large institutional player, we can come along for the ride as the mutual fund or hedge fund continues to push the stock up with its buying, or go down with its selling.
